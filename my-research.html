Copyright, Creative Common (CC) Licenses, Fair Use

Copyright, Creative Common (CC) Licenses, Fair Use

Copyright, Creative Commons
Copyright is an official kind of protection, that is given to the creator of an original work – this includes written, performed (acted or musical), and many other kinds of “intellectual works”.

Copyright laws give the creator of an original work the exclusive rights to use, reproduce, or distribute their work.
(Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment, n.d.)

With the introduction of Creative Commons (CC) licenses, creators can now specify how others can use their work.

There are 4 primary types of CC licenses, listed below:

1. Attribution (BY):
Others are permitted to remix, adapt, build upon or distribute the original creators work (including for commercial gains) so long as the original creator is credited as such.

2. Share Alike(SA):
Others can remix, adapt, build upon, or distribute (NOT for commercial gains) so long as they license their new creations under identical terms.

3. Non Commercial(NC):
Others can remix, adapt, build upon, or distribute (NOT for commercial gains) so long as their works acknowledge the original creator. The new works do not have to be licensed under identical terms.

4. No Derivatives(ND):
Others can reuse the original work for any purpose, but the original work cannot be adapted in any way, and the original creator must be credited accordingly.
(University of Auckland, n.d.)

Fair Use
Fair Use is a legally binding doctrine that permits a limited range of uses of copyrighted materials. This means that permission does not necessarily need to be acquired from the copyrights holders before use.

This is particularly useful where education, news, and research is concerned.

Factors that play a role in determining fair use include:
1. Purpose of the use
2. The nature of the copyrighted work
3. The total portion of the original work used
4. The effect on the value of the original work.
(Copyright Licensing NZ, 2020)

Privacy & Web Privacy Policy

Privacy & Privacy Policy

Privacy is a fundamental human right, ensuring that an individual’s personal information is protected from access by unauthorized entities. In the modern age of the internet, privacy has become increasingly important as businesses and organizations collect vast amounts of information from users across the globe.

A website is required to have a Privacy Policy, which is a legal document that binds the website's owners to a strict guideline outlining how they collect, use, and safeguard any personal information gathered from users. Below is an overview of the New Zealand Privacy Act 2020's 13 Privacy Principles, which provide a framework for handling personal information.

13 Privacy Principles

1. Purpose of Collection:
Personal information should only be collected for a lawful and necessary purpose.

2. Source of Information:
Personal information should be collected directly from the individual unless authorized otherwise.

3. Collection Notification:
The individual must be informed about the purpose of collecting their information and how it will be used.

4. Manner of Collection:
Personal information must be collected lawfully and fairly.

5. Storage and Security:
Agencies must ensure personal information is stored securely and protected from loss, unauthorized access, or misuse.

6. Access to Information:
Individuals have the right to access any personal information held about them.

7. Correction of Information:
Individuals have the right to request correction of any inaccurate information.

8. Accuracy of Information:
Agencies must take reasonable steps to ensure the information they hold is accurate and up-to-date.

9. Retention of Information:
Personal information must not be kept longer than necessary for the purpose it was collected.

10. Use of Information:
Personal information can only be used for the purpose it was collected unless an exception applies.

11. Disclosure of Information:
Personal information should not be disclosed to other entities unless authorized by the individual or required by law.

12. Unique Identifiers:
Agencies should avoid assigning unique identifiers unless necessary to carry out their functions.

13. Cross-border Disclosure:
When disclosing information to overseas entities, agencies must ensure the recipient protects the information as well as the NZ Privacy Act requires.

Any privacy policy should (at a minimum) clearly state what information is being collected from users (such as name, email address, etc).
It should also state how the personal information is being used, and who it will be shared with (if at all).

The privacy policy must also explain to users how they can control their personal data, such as opting out of the collection of their data or posting a request to have their personal information deleted.

(Digital.govt.nz, n.d.)
(Privacy Commissioner, n.d.)
(Consumer, n.d.)

How to improve your website SEO?

Search Engine Optimization (SEO)

Search Engine Optimization (abbreviated to SEO) is the way your website can be optimized to improve the visibility of your website in search engine results (such as google).

Effective Search Engine Optimization is directly attributed to increased traffic on a website, and therefore increased awareness of a brand or service.

There are many key strategies to enhance a websites SEO:

1. Key word optimization:
Incorporating relevant keywords (directly related to your website’s content) in your websites titles, headings, and bodies of text will help users find your website.

2. High Quality Content:
Content that is informative or valuable to your target audience is more likely to be shared (or linked) to other users/websites, which increases your websites presence on the internet.

3. Website Speed:
A website that loads quickly will be favoured in a search engines “rank” – your page will be prioritized if it is more responsive than competitors.
Optimizing the size and quality of images and text will directly effect how fast your page responds to user interactions.

4. Mobile Friendliness:
Considering the vast amount of users now utilizing mobile technology, it is imperative that a website is able to display properly on mobile devices.
Responsive designs that work on all screen sizes is crucial to the usability of a website by all users.
Google priotizies mobile-friendly sites in its rankings during a search.

5. Backlinks:
A website featuring a series of quality links from reputable websites builds trustworthiness, and again increases a websites presence on the internet.

Choosing the right web hosting provider

Web Hosting

Choosing the right web hosting provider is paramount to the success of a website.
A hosting provider affects your website's performance, responsiveness, reliability, and scalability.
Below are 5 factors to consider when selecting a web hosting provider:

1. Reliability:
A web hosting provider must guarantee a high uptime (as close to 100% as possible) to ensure that a website is always accessible.
Downtime results in lost revenue, brand awareness, credibility, and above all, a poor user experience.

2. Customer Support:
Quality customer support is essential when evaluating the usefulness of a web hosting provider. Providers that offer 24/7 support through multiple channels (chat, email, phone) are preferred. This determines how quickly an issue can be resolved, minimizing any disruptions.

3. Scalability:
As a website increases in both content size and user base, more resources will be needed. A web hosting provider that offers scalable plans allows websites to upgrade or downgrade as needed, without significant disruption to users.

4. Security:
A web hosting provider must have strong security measures to protect websites from hacking, malware, and data breaches. Features like SSL certificates, regular backups, and firewall protection should be standard to ensure the safety of user data and website functionality.

5. Pricing and Value for Money:
It's important to choose a hosting provider that offers a balance between cost and features. While cheaper plans might be attractive, they often come with limited resources or support. Assessing the features offered for the price will help you ensure you're getting the best value for your needs.

Web Performance and Maintenance

Web Performance & Maintenance

Web performance is about how fast a website loads and runs on any given user’s device.
Good performance is important to retain visitors and ensure a positive experience for users.
Regular maintenance of a website ensures that it remains functional, secure, and up-to-date.
Below are some methods to ensure good web performance:

1. Optimized Images:
Large images can slow down website loading times. Use compression techniques to reduce file size without compromising quality, and choose appropriate formats such as JPEG or PNG.

2. Minify CSS and JavaScript:
Minification removes unnecessary characters (such as spaces, comments) from CSS and JavaScript files, reducing file sizes and speeding up page load times.

3. Enable Browser Caching:
Browser caching stores elements of a website on a user’s device, allowing the site to load faster when revisited by retrieving stored data instead of reloading all content.

4. Use a Content Delivery Network (CDN):
A CDN stores website files across multiple servers around the globe, allowing users to access content from the server closest to them, reducing latency and loading times.

5. Reduce HTTP Requests:
Minimizing the number of HTTP requests by combining CSS and JavaScript files, as well as limiting the number of images, can significantly reduce load times.

(Shopify, n.d.)

In addition to performance improvements, regular maintenance is crucial to keep the website functional and secure. Below are 5 important maintenance tasks:

1. Backup Regularly:
A regular backup of all website data will ensure that a website can be restored to a previous state, in the case where data loss or an attack takes place.

2. Update Software and Plugins:
By keeping a websites content management system and plugins up-to-date ensures security patches and performance improvements are applied regularly.

3. Monitor for Broken Links:
A regular check for and (and fix of) broken links will improve overall user experience and search engine optimization (SEO). Broken links can frustrate users and negatively impact search rankings.

4. Check Website Speed:
A periodic test of a websites loading speed (using tools like Google PageSpeed Insights) ensures that performance stays optimal, particularly as content grows.

5. Security Audits:
By performing regular security checks (including scanning for malware and vulnerabilities) ensures that a website remains secure and protected from potential attacks.

Web Security

Web Security

Web security is one of the most important aspects where maintaining a safe and trustworthy website is concerned.
Websites are often the target of various “cyber-attacks” – each with its own unique methods of attack and degrees of impact on both users and data.

Below are some of the more common kinds of cyber-attacks, and how to go about preventing them:

1. Phishing Attacks:
Phishing attacks occur when a user is fooled into providing sensitive information (e.g. password or credit card details) to an untrustworthy entity. This attack is performed by a website (or email agent) that pretends to represent a verified company/entity.

To prevent Phishing attacks, a user can implement a robust approach to email filtering, employing a system to block “suspicious messages”, and also to conduct/attend user education programmes to help users raise awareness about (and recognize) an attempted phishing attack.

2. SQL Injection:
SQL Injection is a form of cyber-attack where malicious SQL code is ran as a query, to manipulated a targeted database and gain (usually) unauthorized access to sensitive data.

To prevent SQL Injection attacks, the use of strong input validation will ensure that only valid data is accepted and processed.

3. Cross-Site Scripting (XSS):
XSS Attacks involve the injection of a malicious script(s) into a target webpage. The aim is usually to either steal data or deface the website. This is a similar attack to a SQL Injection attack.

To prevent XSS attacks, the adoption of secure coding practices is paramount to preventing the injection of malicious code. This can be done by:

- Sanitizing user inputs - Escaping special characters - Using content security policies (CSP) to block malicious scripts.

4. Distributed Denial of Service (DDOS):
DDOS attacks flood a server with an overwhelming volume of traffic, which renders a website or service inoperable and therefore unavailable to other users.

To prevent DDOS attacks, the implementation of a traffic monitoring system designed to detect unusual spikes and deploy a CDN (content delivery network) will distribute traffic across multiple servers. This will absorb any sudden traffic surges, rendering a DDOS ineffective.

5. Malware:
Malware often refers to a kind of malicious software (often called a “virus”) that can and will harm or exploit systems, users, sensitive data, and aid in the gaining of unauthorized access to networks or private information.

Malware attacks can be prevented by using reliable security software(s).
This software will detect and remove malware, keep malware-detection systems, applications and libraries updated to patch over vulnerabilities, and regularly monitor networks for “suspicious” activities and/or unusual behaviour from programs/services.
